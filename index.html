    Gemini for Recruiters & Sourcers

    From Intake to Search Strategy

    Turn messy intake notes, vague job descriptions, and scattered hiring manager feedback into a clear, sourcing-ready search strategy using Gemini in Google Workspace.

    Step 1 of 8

    Start with the messy intake

    Here is what you usually get.

    Reality

    Rarely a clean, sourcing-ready brief

    What you usually get is a mix of:

    • a job description that sounds polished but generic
    • rushed intake notes
    • hiring manager comments
    • internal assumptions
    • half-formed priorities
    • contradictions between what was written and what was actually said
    Reframe the goal

    The goal is not to trust the intake at face value. The goal is to capture it as raw material. This is where the workflow starts.

    Example intake (copy this to follow along) 
    "We need a senior product security person."
"Must be hands-on, but also strategic."
"Someone from a fast-moving startup would be ideal."
"Big company experience could also work."
"Needs to work closely with engineering."
"This person should influence security culture."
"We want strong communication skills."
"Cloud background is important."
"Ideally someone who has seen scale."
"We don't want someone too compliance-heavy."
    Teaching point

    At this stage, do not force clarity too early. First, collect the raw signal. Your job in Step 1 is to gather the notes, not to solve the role yet.

    Step 2 of 8

    Clean the signal

    Use Gemini to separate facts from noise.

    The move

    Classify, do not rewrite

    Instead of asking Gemini to "rewrite the notes nicely," ask it to classify the information. You want it to separate confirmed facts, assumptions, ambiguous statements, contradictions, and open questions. That gives you a more honest starting point.

    Prompt 
    I'm going to paste intake notes for a recruiting search. Please organize them
into 5 sections:

1. Confirmed facts
2. Assumptions
3. Ambiguous or vague statements
4. Contradictions or tension points
5. Open questions that need clarification

Do not invent missing information. Keep the wording concise and practical.
    Example output

    What Gemini should produce

    • Confirmed Senior role; works closely with engineering; cloud background important; wants hands-on
    • Assumptions "Strategic" may mean influencing direction; "fast-moving startup" may signal pace tolerance
    • Ambiguous "seen scale", "strong communication", "influence security culture"
    • Contradictions Startup profile preferred, but big company experience also valued; hands-on vs strategic
    • Open questions What cloud environment? Leadership influence vs technical ownership? What does "too compliance-heavy" exclude?
    Teaching point

    Cleaning the signal is the difference between reacting to words and understanding the search.

    Step 3 of 8

    Extract the real must-haves

    Stop treating the JD as truth.

    The trap

    Not every line is a must-have

    Most roles contain real must-haves, useful but flexible preferences, generic filler, and signals that sound important but will not decide success. Your goal is to identify the 2 to 4 criteria that genuinely determine fit.

    Prompt 
    Based on the intake notes below, identify:

1. Real must-haves (max 4)
2. Nice-to-haves
3. Generic or low-signal requirements

For each must-have, explain why it is likely to matter in the actual search.
Be strict. Do not treat every preference as a must-have.
    Example output

    Strict separation

    • Must-haves hands-on product/security experience; works directly with engineering; cloud security exposure; fast-moving environments
    • Nice-to-have experience at scale; cross-functional communication; startup experience; big-company background if execution-oriented
    • Low-signal "strategic", "strong communication skills", "influence culture"
    Teaching point

    Must-haves should narrow the search. If your must-haves still describe half the market, they are not real must-haves.

    Step 4 of 8

    Translate feedback into sourcing implications

    What this feedback actually changes.

    The shift

    Summaries do not improve the search

    Feedback often arrives vague: "not senior enough", "too corporate", "we want someone more strategic". If you only summarize it, the search does not improve. Translate it into what to tighten, what to loosen, what to stop prioritizing, what to add, and what false positives to avoid.

    Example feedback (copy to try) 
    "This person is strong technically, but feels too infrastructure-heavy."
"We want someone closer to product."
"Good background, but maybe too process-oriented."
"We need more influence and judgment."
    Prompt 
    Here is hiring manager feedback on candidate profiles. Translate it into sourcing
implications using this format:

1. What this really means
2. What to tighten
3. What to loosen
4. What profile patterns to prioritize
5. What profile patterns to avoid
6. Updated search hypothesis

Be practical. Focus on how the search should change.
    Example output

    Implications, not notes

    • Means not a broad infrastructure profile; closer to product decisions and engineering collaboration
    • Tighten product security exposure; proximity to product and application teams; judgment over process
    • Loosen pedigree from large formal security orgs; heavy process/compliance emphasis
    • Prioritize product security engineers; security architects with application focus
    • Avoid infrastructure-only backgrounds; compliance-heavy or governance-heavy profiles
    • Hypothesis product-facing security people who operate hands-on while influencing engineering and product
    Teaching point

    Feedback should update the search logic, not just the notes.

    Step 5 of 8

    Build the target profile

    Who you should actually search for.

    Make it searchable

    A strong target profile answers

    • what titles are most likely relevant
    • what types of companies to target
    • what team setup this person likely comes from
    • what work signals matter
    • what false positives look close but are weak fits
    Prompt 
    Based on the intake notes and feedback, create a target candidate profile
for sourcing. Include:

1. likely current titles
2. likely background
3. likely company types
4. strong work signals
5. weak signals / false positives
6. possible adjacent profiles worth testing

Keep it practical and sourcing-oriented.
    Example output

    A searchable picture

    • Titles Senior Product Security Engineer; Product Security Lead; Application Security Lead; Security Architect (product-facing)
    • Background worked with engineering and product; secured applications or cloud-native systems; balances execution and influence
    • Companies B2B SaaS; cloud-native product companies; high-growth, product-led orgs
    • Strong signals built product security practices; embedded with engineering; influenced secure development
    • False positives infra-heavy security without product exposure; governance/compliance-heavy; far from execution
    • Adjacent senior application security engineers; platform security with product partnership
    Teaching point

    The target profile should help you search better, not just describe the ideal person in abstract terms.

    Step 6 of 8

    Produce the sourcing brief

    A usable output for real search work.

    Operational output

    A working search document

    This is where the workflow becomes operational. The brief should be more than a cleaned-up summary. It should help you or your team act immediately.

    Recommended structure

    Thirteen sections

    • Role Summary, Confirmed Facts, Real Must-Haves, Nice-to-Haves
    • Target Profile, Strong Signals, Weak Signals / False Positives
    • Company Clusters to Search, Company Types to Avoid
    • Search Terms, Outreach Angle, Calibration Risks
    • Recommended First Search Strategy
    Prompt 
    Turn the analysis into a sourcing-ready search brief. Use the following
sections:

1. Role Summary
2. Confirmed Facts
3. Real Must-Haves
4. Nice-to-Haves
5. Target Profile
6. Strong Signals
7. Weak Signals / False Positives
8. Company Clusters to Search
9. Company Types to Avoid
10. Search Terms
11. Outreach Angle
12. Calibration Risks
13. Recommended First Search Strategy

Keep it concise, practical, and directly usable by a recruiter or sourcer.
    Teaching point

    If the output does not help someone start searching, it is not a real sourcing brief yet.

    Make it repeatable

    Turn this into your standard workflow.

    Standardize what works

    The value is not doing this once. It is turning it into a repeatable operating system. Create a repeatable intake format, a reusable prompt structure, a standard output template, a review checklist, and a home for it in Google Docs or Drive.

    Input

    Step 8 of 8

    Turn the workflow into a Gem

    Do not rebuild the process every time.

    Why a Gem

    Save the workflow, reuse it on every role

    If you re-explain the same recruiting workflow every time, you do not have a real system yet. A Gem lets you save the workflow logic and reuse it whenever a new role, a new intake, or new feedback comes in. One recruiting Gem that knows how to clean intake, separate facts from assumptions, find must-haves, interpret feedback, build a target profile, and produce a sourcing-ready brief.

    The distinction

    A workflow is the process. A Gem is the reusable wrapper for that process.

    1 · What the Gem should do

    A recruiting workflow assistant

    Its job is not to rewrite the notes nicely. Its job is to turn messy recruiting materials into a practical, sourcing-ready search strategy. It should help with:

    • role intake analysis
    • search calibration
    • hiring manager feedback interpretation
    • target profile definition
    • sourcing brief creation
    Name and description

    Recruiting Intake-to-Search Strategist

    Description: Transforms messy recruiting intake materials into a sourcing-ready search strategy for recruiters and sourcers.

    Gem instructions (paste into your Gem) 
    You are a recruiting and sourcing workflow assistant.

Your role is to help recruiters and sourcers turn messy intake materials into a clear, sourcing-ready search strategy.

You may receive:
- job descriptions
- intake notes
- hiring manager notes
- hiring manager feedback
- internal recruiting comments
- market notes
- candidate profile reactions

Your job is not to rewrite the input nicely.
Your job is to structure the signal, identify what matters, and turn the material into practical recruiting output.

Always follow this workflow:

Step 1: Clean the signal
Separate the input into:
- Confirmed facts
- Assumptions
- Ambiguous or vague statements
- Contradictions or tension points
- Open questions

Step 2: Extract the real must-haves
Identify:
- Real must-haves (maximum 2 to 4)
- Nice-to-haves
- Generic or low-signal requirements
Be strict. Do not treat every preference as a must-have.

Step 3: Translate feedback into sourcing implications
If feedback is provided, explain:
- What this really means
- What to tighten
- What to loosen
- What profile patterns to prioritize
- What profile patterns to avoid
- Updated search hypothesis

Step 4: Build the target profile
Create a sourcing-oriented target profile that includes:
- Likely current titles
- Likely background
- Likely company types
- Strong work signals
- Weak signals / false positives
- Adjacent profiles worth testing

Step 5: Produce the sourcing brief
Always return a final sourcing-ready brief using this structure:
# Role Summary
## Confirmed Facts
## Real Must-Haves
## Nice-to-Haves
## Target Profile
## Strong Signals
## Weak Signals / False Positives
## Company Clusters to Search
## Company Types to Avoid
## Search Terms
## Outreach Angle
## Calibration Risks
## Recommended First Search Strategy

Rules:
- Do not invent missing information.
- If something is unclear, label it as an open question or assumption.
- Treat job descriptions as inputs, not as truth.
- Optimize for practical recruiting and sourcing use, not polished generic writing.
- Keep outputs concise, structured, and directly usable.
- If the input is too vague, say so clearly.
- If feedback contradicts the original role direction, point it out explicitly.
- Separate facts, assumptions, risks, and recommendations whenever useful.

Your output should help a recruiter or sourcer act immediately.
If the result is too generic to guide a real search, improve specificity.

    1. Open GeminiSign in to your Google account in the browser.

    4 · How to review whether it worked

    Quick review checklist

    • Did it separate facts from assumptions?
    • Are the must-haves truly selective?
    • Did it flag what is vague or contradictory?
    • Did feedback actually change the strategy?
    • Is the target profile searchable?
    • Are false positives clearly defined?
    • Could a sourcer start working from this brief immediately?

    If not, refine the input or ask the Gem to tighten the output.

    Bonus

    Common mistakes to avoid

    Five ways this workflow goes wrong.

    1

    Treating the JD as the truth

    The JD is usually a starting point, not a sourcing strategy.

    2

    Asking Gemini to "improve the notes"

    That often creates cleaner text, but not better search logic.

    3

    Turning every preference into a must-have

    This makes the search unrealistic and unfocused.

    4

    Summarizing feedback without changing the search

    Feedback should change priorities, not just wording.

    5

    Accepting polished output without checking usefulness

    A sourcing brief should help you search, calibrate, and act.
